The New World's hot, tropical weather was seen as a new ideal for the cultivation of coffee, and coffee plantations quickly spread throughout Central America, with their first coffee harvest occurring in 1726.
Latin American countries also have ideal coffee-growing conditions, such as a good balance of sunshine and rain, high and humid temperatures, and rich soil. By the mid-18th century, Latin American countries evolved into being some of the top coffee producers, able to clear big tropical forests, ready for plantations. And today, Brazil is the world's biggest coffee producer.

Origin and Evolution

Coffee finds its roots in the highlands of Ethiopia, where a goat herder first discovered its stimulating effects. Today, Arabica and robusta beans flourish in diverse climates, from Colombia's mountains to Vietnam's lush fields. The art of roasting transforms these beans into a spectrum of flavors and aromas, from bright and acidic to deep and chocolatey.
